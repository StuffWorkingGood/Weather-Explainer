Almost every number a forecast shows you means something different from what it sounds like. Tap any card to see what it actually measures.
Humidity
64%
Relative humidity, not the amount of water in the air

The word people skip is relative. 64% does not mean the air is 64% water. It means the air is holding 64% of the maximum water vapor it could hold at the current temperature.

Warm air can hold far more water than cold air, roughly 7% more for every degree Celsius. So the same actual moisture shows a totally different humidity number depending on temperature. This is why humidity climbs at night as the air cools, even though no water was added.

Drag the air temperature below. The amount of water in the air stays fixed the whole time. Only the temperature changes.

Dew Point
70°F
The honest moisture number the pros actually watch

Dew point is the temperature the air would need to cool to before water starts condensing out. Unlike relative humidity, it does not swing around with temperature, so it is a straight readout of how much water is actually in the air.

This is why meteorologists trust it over humidity for judging how muggy it feels. A 90°F day at 40% humidity and a 75°F day at 70% humidity can carry the same dew point and feel equally sticky.

Chance of Rain
40%
Not "40% chance it rains on you." It is a multiplication.

This is the most misread number in the whole forecast. The official name is Probability of Precipitation, and the Weather Service defines it as two things multiplied together:

PoP = Confidence × Coverage. Confidence is how sure the forecaster is that rain happens somewhere in the area. Coverage is the fraction of the area expected to actually get wet if it does.

So a 40% chance can come from very different situations. Drag both sliders and watch how they land on the same number.

Confidence rain occurs80%
Area that gets wet50%
80%×50%=40%
"40%" might mean a forecaster is 100% sure it rains, just only over 40% of the map. Or 40% sure it rains at all, but everywhere if it does. Same headline number, completely different day. And "measurable" only means at least 0.01 inch, so a drizzle counts.
Feels Like
92°F
Your body's cooling math, not the actual air temperature

The thermometer reads 84°F, but "feels like" says 92°F. Nothing is wrong with either. Feels-like is a model of how well your body can shed heat, not a reading of the air.

In heat, your main cooling tool is sweat evaporating off your skin. When humidity is high, the air is already near saturated, so sweat evaporates slowly and you cannot dump heat. The heat index converts that stalled cooling into an equivalent dry temperature.

In cold, the same idea runs backward as wind chill: wind strips the thin warm layer off your skin and hauls heat away faster, so it feels colder than the air.

Pressure
29.9in
Steady

The absolute value barely matters day to day. What forecasters watch is the direction it is moving. Falling pressure means a low-pressure system is moving in, which pulls air upward, cools it, and builds clouds and storms.

Rising pressure pushes air down and dries it out, which is why "the barometer is climbing" is old-school shorthand for clearing skies.

Around 29.92 in is average. The trend arrow is the real forecast, not the number.
UV Index
7
High

Not a temperature and not tied to how hot it feels. It is a scale of how fast UV radiation damages skin, running 0 to 11+. A cool, clear, high-altitude day can carry a brutal UV number.

It is roughly linear: a 7 burns you about twice as fast as a 3.5. Peak values land near solar noon, not in the afternoon heat.

The value tracks the sun's angle and altitude, not the thermometer. Snow and water bounce it back up at you too.
Wind
9mph
Gusts 22

The main number is the sustained wind, a roughly two-minute average. The gust is the short peak, and the gap between them is what actually matters. A steady 9 with gusts to 22 is a lumpy, unpredictable day even though the average sounds calm.

The average tells you the trend. The gust tells you whether your umbrella survives.
Visibility
10mi

Ten miles is usually the ceiling the instrument reports, not a promise you can see ten miles. It measures how far light gets through the air before haze, fog, dust, or rain scatters it.

"10 mi" often just means "clear enough that we stopped counting."
The 10-Day Forecast
Day 8?
Confidence does not last as long as the row of icons suggests

Those ten cheerful icons imply equal certainty across the row. They are not equal. A day-one forecast is right around 95% of the time. By day five it drops toward the high 80s, and past about a week it is closer to an educated guess than a forecast.

The atmosphere is chaotic, so tiny errors in today's measurements grow fast. That is the real reason the 10-day keeps changing every time you check it.

Trust roughly the first five icons. Treat the back half as a rough outlook that will quietly rewrite itself before it arrives.
